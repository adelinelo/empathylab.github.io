<h2 style="color: #2e6c80;">Claire L. Adida</h2>

<blockquote>

<p>Claire Adida is an Associate Professor of Political Science at the University of California, San Diego. She uses quantitative methods to study how countries manage new and existing forms of diversity. She has applied this question to the study of immigrant exclusion and ethnic politics in urban West Africa, France, and the United States. Her current research investigates the conditions that reduce out-group discrimination, and the strategies vulnerable minorities employ to navigate discriminatory environments. Her work is funded by the National Science Foundation, the Bill &amp; Melinda Gates Foundation, the Hellman Foundation, and the Evidence in Governance and Politics Group.</p>

<p>Email: <a href="mailto:cadida@ucsd.edu">cadida@ucsd.edu</a> <a href="https://claire.adida.net/">website</a></p>

</blockquote>

<h2 style="color: #2e6c80;">Adeline Y. Lo</h2>

<blockquote>

<p>Adeline Lo is an Assistant Professor in the Department of Political Science at the University of Wisconsin-Madison and is the Glenn B. &amp; Cleone Orr Hawkins Chair of Political Science. She researches the factors that motivate or mitigate conflict between groups, with an eye towards investigating migrant inclusionary politics. She also designs statistical tools for prediction and measurement for applied social science data. Her research has led her to work with and construct tools for observational, text, experimental and network data. She received her Ph.D in Political Science from the University of California, San Diego in 2016, her M.A. in Politics from New York University in 2011, and her B.A. in Economics and Political Science from Columbia University in 2008.</p>

<p>Email: <a href="mailto:cadida@ucsd.edu">aylo@wisc.edu</a> <a href="https://loadeline.com">website</a></p>

</blockquote>

<h2 style="color: #2e6c80;">Melina R. Platas</h2>

<blockquote>

<p>Melina Platas <a href="https://nyuad.nyu.edu/en/academics/faculty/melina-platas.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Assistant Professor of Political Science</a>&nbsp;at&nbsp;<a href="http://nyuad.nyu.edu/en/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">New York University Abu Dhabi</a>. She studies the politics of development, organized around three themes: 1) culture and development, 2) information, accountability, and service delivery, and 3) migration. Her book project, <em><a href="https://melinaplatas.com/dissertationbook-project/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Culture and the Persistence of Inequality</a></em>, presents the colonial roots of educational inequality across religious groups in Africa, and the cultural factors that have allowed inequality to persist. She employs a variety of methods, ranging from field experiments to ethnography. The regional focus of her research is sub-Saharan Africa, where she has worked and conducted fieldwork in eight countries since 2005. She received a PhD in political science from Stanford University in 2016, and a BA in Human Biology from Stanford University in 2007 (go Card!).</p>

<p>Email: <a href="mailto:cadida@ucsd.edu">mplatas@nyu.edu</a> <a href="https://melinaplatas.com/">website</a></p>

</blockquote>

<h2 style="color: #2e6c80;">Scott Williamson</h2>

<blockquote>

<p class="">Scott Williamson is an Assistant Professor in the Department of Social and Political Sciences at Bocconi University. He completed his PhD in political science at Stanford University in 2020. His primary research agenda explores the factors that sustain authoritarian rule, particularly in the Middle East. He is especially interested in how institutions stabilize autocracies by shaping public opinion toward accountability and democracy.</p>
